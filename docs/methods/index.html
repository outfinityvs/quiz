Methods

Outfinity Quiz hosts four types of instruments. Each type has different origins, validation status, and appropriate uses. This page explains how they work and what their results mean.

Instrument Types

Research Instrument

A published instrument from academic or professional literature, used according to its original method and license. The scoring algorithm reproduces the published methodology. Results should be interpreted within the context of the original research.

Derived Map

Based on open scales or published instruments, but with an interpretation layer developed by Outfinity. The scoring may follow the original methodology, but the result framing, archetypes, and recommendations are experimental. These instruments are labeled clearly.

Reflective Exploration

An original self-knowledge instrument designed by Outfinity. These are not validated psychometric assessments. They are designed to provoke reflection, surface patterns, and offer a vocabulary for self-understanding. Results are exploratory, not diagnostic.

Interactive Fiction

A narrative experience that places you in scenarios and asks you to make choices. The result is an archetype or classification based on your responses. These are not psychological evaluations. They are designed to be thought-provoking and shareable.

Scoring

All scoring happens in your browser. No data is transmitted. The engine supports multiple scoring strategies:

  • Sum and mean: Adding or averaging item scores within a dimension.
  • Reverse scoring: Some items are scored inversely to detect response patterns.
  • Subscales: Groups of items that measure a sub-dimension, combined into a broader scale.
  • Prototype distance: Comparing your profile to predefined archetypes using Euclidean distance.
  • Geometric mean: Used when all dimensions must be present (a low score in any dimension reduces the overall result).
  • Threshold classification: Placing results into categories based on score cutoffs.
  • Forced-choice weighting: First and second preferences receive different point values.

Result Interpretation

Results are expressed as scores on axes, normalized to 0–100. A score of 75 on an axis does not mean "75% of people score lower." It means the dimension is relatively strong in your profile compared to other dimensions.

Every result includes:

  • Profile: A title and synthesis of your result.
  • Map: Your scores on each dimension.
  • Contribution: Where you are likely to produce value.
  • Vulnerability: How your strengths may distort or become counterproductive.
  • Environment: Contexts where your profile functions well.
  • Anti-environment: Contexts that amplify your weaknesses.
  • Complementarity: Roles or profiles that complete yours.
  • Action: A concrete experiment for the next 7–30 days.
  • Limitations: How the result may be wrong or incomplete.

What Results Are Not

Results are not clinical diagnoses, predictions of success, rankings of human value, or admissions criteria. No profile is superior to another. Some combinations are better suited to certain environments.

If a result resonates, use it as a starting point for reflection or conversation. If it does not, consider whether the instrument was the right one for your current question.
